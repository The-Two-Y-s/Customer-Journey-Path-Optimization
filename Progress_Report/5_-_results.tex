\chapter{EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS AND ANALYSIS}\label{chap:results}

This chapter presents the results of the full experimental evaluation and evaluates the central hypothesis: whether probability-based pruning can deliver meaningful speedup without sacrificing path optimality. Section~\ref{sec:synth_results} covers the 2,160-row synthetic benchmark; Section~\ref{sec:real_results} covers the 300-row real-data validation on RetailRocket and RecSys~2015.

Speedups scale monotonically with $\tau$, ranging from $3.3\times$ wall-clock at $\tau=0.001$ to $738\times$ at $\tau=0.5$. The ``Edge Speedup'' column---based on \texttt{edges\_relaxed}---consistently exceeds both other measures because its pruned denominator excludes threshold-pruned edges; it quantifies pruning aggressiveness, not raw work reduction. The ``Exam.\ Speedup'' column provides the fair apples-to-apples comparison using \texttt{edges\_examined}, which counts every outgoing edge the algorithm inspects regardless of whether it survives the threshold (see Table~\ref{table:edges_relaxed} for counter semantics). At $\tau=0.5$, the examination speedup ($711\times$) closely tracks wall-clock speedup ($738\times$), confirming that the wall-clock gains are genuine and not inflated by counter semantics. At lower $\tau$ values (e.g.\ $\tau=0.001$, exam.\ speedup $1.9\times$, wall-clock $3.3\times$), the pruned algorithm still examines most edges but skips many relaxation operations, and Python per-call overhead becomes a larger fraction of the reduced runtime. Across all 178 path-found rows among the 1,800 pruned synthetic runs ($\tau > 0$), the optimality gap remained exactly $\Delta = 0.0000\%$, confirming the all-or-nothing property (Theorem~\ref{thm:all_or_nothing}, Chapter~\ref{chap:correctness}). Path-found rates decrease with $\tau$, reflecting the reachability--speed trade-off inherent in the pruning approach.

Figure~\ref{fig:speedup_vs_tau} plots the wall-clock speedup ratio (baseline time divided by pruned time) against the pruning threshold $\tau$ on a logarithmic $x$-axis spanning $\tau \in [0.001, 0.5]$. Erd\H{o}s--R\'{e}nyi graphs consistently achieve higher speedups than layered graphs because their uniformly random connectivity exposes a larger fraction of low-probability edges to the threshold. In contrast, the layered generator's built-in forward bias toward the target means that many edges carry relatively high probability and therefore survive pruning. Larger graphs (higher $|V|$) produce steeper speedup curves, reflecting the greater absolute fraction of the search space that the threshold eliminates as the graph grows.

This constant-memory behaviour makes the pruned algorithm particularly attractive for deployment on memory-constrained systems.

\subsection{Optimality Gap Analysis}

A critical finding is the \textbf{zero optimality gap} across all experiments:

\[
\Delta = \frac{|C^*_{\text{baseline}} - C^*_{\text{pruned}}|}{C^*_{\text{baseline}}} \times 100\% = 0.0000\% \quad \forall\, \text{path-found rows}
\]

This confirms Theorem~\ref{thm:all_or_nothing} (All-or-Nothing): whenever the pruned algorithm returns a path, it is the globally optimal path. The cost-based gap formula (not probability-based) eliminates floating-point rounding noise involved in $\exp(-C^*)$ conversion. Using probability-based differences would have introduced \texttt{1e-15}-scale noise that has no algorithmic significance.

For the \textbf{RetailRocket event-level} configuration, the 3-node funnel graph is sufficiently small that both algorithms complete in microseconds; 78 of 100 configurations found paths and speedup is minimal ($\leq 6\times$) because there is almost nothing to prune. The near-zero path-found rates on the large graphs are \emph{not} surprising once the baseline path probabilities are examined. For RecSys~2015, the 20~baseline path probabilities range from $1.6 \times 10^{-8}$ to $2.8 \times 10^{-5}$ (median $\approx 1.5 \times 10^{-6}$), yielding costs in the range 10.5--17.9 on the $-\log$ scale. Even the most conservative fixed threshold $\tau = 0.0001$ maps to a cost threshold of $-\log(0.0001) = 9.21$---well below every baseline cost. All 20~paths are therefore \emph{mathematically guaranteed} to be pruned at every tested $\tau$, regardless of graph structure (by Theorem~\ref{thm:all_or_nothing}). RetailRocket item-level shows a similar pattern: baseline probabilities range from $2.9 \times 10^{-9}$ to $3.7 \times 10^{-4}$ (median $\approx 3.7 \times 10^{-6}$), and only one pair's cost falls below $-\log(0.0001)$. These observations motivate the adaptive-$\tau$ experiment described below.

\subsection{Adaptive-$\tau$ Experiment}\label{sec:adaptive_tau}

The fixed $\tau$ grid was designed for the synthetic experiments (where edge probabilities are higher) and is poorly calibrated for real-world item-level graphs whose cumulative path probabilities are orders of magnitude smaller. To address this, we added a per-pair \emph{adaptive} $\tau$ sweep: for each source--target pair, $\tau$ is set to a fraction $f \in \{0.10, 0.30, 0.50, 0.70, 0.90, 0.95, 0.99, 1.00, 1.10\}$ of that pair's baseline path probability. This mirrors the adaptive sweep strategy used internally by the \texttt{critical\_tau} module (Chapter~\ref{chap:methodology}, Section~\ref{sec:critical_tau}) and ensures that the tested thresholds are always in the relevant probability regime.

RetailRocket (item-level)   &     1.2$\times$ &   3.4$\times$ & 152 / 180 & 0.000\% \\

The contrast with the fixed-$\tau$ results is striking. RecSys~2015 moves from 0/100 found paths (fixed) to \textbf{150/180} found paths (adaptive); RetailRocket item-level similarly improves from 1/100 to \textbf{152/180}. The speedups under adaptive $\tau$ are modest (1.1--5.8$\times$) because the threshold is now close to the optimal path probability, so only a small margin of the search space is pruned. This is the expected behaviour of the reachability--speed trade-off: in the adaptive regime the algorithm preserves the optimal path at the cost of reduced pruning, whereas the fixed-$\tau$ regime achieves massive speedups only when the threshold is far above the optimal path probability (and therefore prunes everything).

Crucially, the optimality gap remains \textbf{exactly $0.0000\%$} across all 449 adaptive found paths, extending the zero-gap guarantee to a total of 528 real-data found paths (79 fixed + 449 adaptive) and reinforcing the all-or-nothing property on real-world graphs.

In synthetic graphs, the BFS connectivity guarantee ensures the baseline always finds a path from $s$ to $t$. For the real-world datasets, the pair-selection procedure filters source--target pairs by baseline reachability, so the baseline always finds a path. Under fixed $\tau$, the low pruned-found rates on item-level and session-level graphs reflect the mismatch between the fixed threshold grid and the extremely low baseline path probabilities (see Section~\ref{sec:adaptive_tau}).

\textbf{Impact of adaptive $\tau$.} The adaptive regime resolves the near-zero reachability problem: path-found rates jump from 1\% to 84\% (RR~item-level) and from 0\% to 83\% (RecSys~2015). The remaining ${\sim}17\%$ of unfound paths correspond to the most aggressive fraction ($f=0.10$), where $\tau$ is set to just 10\% of the baseline probability---well above it on the $-\log$ cost scale---which intentionally prunes the optimal path. For $f \geq 0.90$ (i.e.\ $\tau \leq 0.90 \times p_{\text{baseline}}$), virtually all paths are preserved.

\textbf{Practical implications.} The fixed-$\tau$ results reveal a genuine limitation of using a single global threshold across datasets with vastly different probability regimes. In item-level graphs, per-edge MLE probabilities are extremely low ($\sim10^{-3}$--$10^{-5}$) and path probabilities decay exponentially with length, yielding cumulative probabilities of $10^{-6}$ or less. The adaptive $\tau$ approach---which can be automated via the \texttt{critical\_tau} module---makes the pruned algorithm viable on any graph by calibrating the threshold to the actual probability scale. In production, one baseline query per source--target pair suffices to set $\tau$; the pruned query then runs with a calibrated threshold at negligible additional cost.

\section{Summary of Findings}

The experimental evaluation yields several principal findings. Wall-clock speedups range from $3.3\times$ at $\tau=0.001$ to $738\times$ at $\tau=0.5$ on synthetic data, and reach up to $18{,}882\times$ on real-world item-level graphs under fixed $\tau$. The optimality gap remained at exactly $\Delta = 0.0000\%$ across all 706 experiment rows in which both algorithms found a path---178 of 1,800 pruned synthetic rows, 79 of 300 fixed-$\tau$ real-data rows, and 449 of 540 adaptive-$\tau$ real-data rows---confirming the all-or-nothing property established in Theorem~\ref{thm:all_or_nothing}.

\textbf{Statistical significance.} Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (one-sided, alternative: baseline $>$ pruned) were applied to all 180 unique synthetic configurations (each with 10 paired runs). Of these, 179 tests rejected the null hypothesis at $\alpha = 0.05$, with $p$-values ranging from $9.77 \times 10^{-4}$ (the minimum attainable for $n=10$ paired observations) to $0.032$. The median $p$-value was $9.77 \times 10^{-4}$. One configuration (layered, $|V|=1{,}000$, $d=5$, uniform, $\tau=0.001$) yielded $p=0.116$, failing the significance threshold; this is the mildest pruning level on a small, forward-biased graph where baseline and pruned running times are very close. Excluding this single outlier, all remaining configurations confirm that the speedup is not an artefact of measurement noise.

Speedup grows with graph size---from $9.4\times$ at $|V|=1{,}000$ to $77\times$ at $|V|=10{,}000$ at fixed $\tau=0.01$---because larger graphs contain more pruneable edges. The fair \texttt{edges\_examined}-based examination speedup confirms these gains are genuine (e.g.\ $5.7\times$ to $41.3\times$ over the same size range), while the higher \texttt{edges\_relaxed}-based ratios quantify pruning aggressiveness. At the same time, higher $\tau$ values reduce path-found rates from 30.6\% at $\tau=0.001$ to 4.2\% at $\tau=0.5$ on synthetic data, demonstrating the inherent reachability--speed trade-off. Under fixed $\tau$, real-world item-level and session-level graphs showed near-zero pruned-found rates because baseline path probabilities ($10^{-6}$--$10^{-9}$) fall far below even $\tau=0.0001$. Introducing an adaptive-$\tau$ regime---where the threshold is set as a fraction of each pair's baseline path probability---raised path-found rates to 82--84\% while maintaining zero optimality gap, validating the algorithm's correctness on real-world graphs.

\textbf{Practical business impact.} The peak $18{,}882\times$ speedup on the RetailRocket item-level graph (44,711 items) reduced a single query from ${\sim}153$\,ms to $0.008$\,ms under fixed-$\tau$ pruning. Under adaptive $\tau$, the algorithm trades some of that extreme speedup for guaranteed path recovery (84\% found rate), while still delivering measurable acceleration. For a team running daily journey analysis, this makes the pruned algorithm viable across all probability regimes---from dense event-level funnels (where fixed $\tau$ suffices) to sparse item-level graphs (where adaptive $\tau$ is needed)---supporting real-time exploration of conversion funnels without overnight batch jobs.

\textbf{Critical-$\tau$ analysis.} A detailed critical-$\tau$ analysis, including per-configuration $\tau^*$ heatmaps and practical threshold recommendations, will be presented in the final report following additional experimentation across the full parameter matrix.
